🌍 Section 1 — India's Position & Borders
1India is the _____ largest country in the world by land area.
💡 Explanation
India is the 7th largest country in the world by land area. It covers about 3.29 million sq km, making it larger than Pakistan, France, and many other nations but smaller than Australia, Brazil, China, USA, Canada and Russia.
2India lies entirely in which hemisphere?
💡 Explanation
India lies entirely in the Northern and Eastern hemisphere. It is located north of the equator (Northern hemisphere) and east of the Prime Meridian (Eastern hemisphere).
3What is the longitudinal extent of India?
💡 Explanation
India's longitudinal extent is 68°7'E to 97°25'E — spanning about 29 degrees of longitude. This causes a time difference of nearly 2 hours between India's easternmost and westernmost points.
4What is the latitudinal extent of India?
💡 Explanation
India's latitudinal extent is 8°4'N to 37°6'N — spanning about 29 degrees of latitude. The Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) passes roughly through the middle of India.
5Which country shares the longest boundary with India?
💡 Explanation
Bangladesh shares the longest boundary with India — 4,097 km. This is because Bangladesh is almost entirely surrounded by India on three sides, creating an exceptionally long shared border.
6What is the length of India's boundary with Bangladesh?
💡 Explanation
India's boundary with Bangladesh is 4,097 km — the longest land border India shares with any single country. This reflects the geographic reality that Bangladesh is nearly encircled by Indian territory.
7India's coastline stretches over:
💡 Explanation
India's coastline stretches over 7,500 km — touching the Arabian Sea (west), the Bay of Bengal (east), and the Indian Ocean (south). Including island coastlines, the total is over 15,000 km.
8The Line of Control defines India's border with which country?
💡 Explanation
The Line of Control (LoC) defines the de facto border between India and Pakistan in the Kashmir region. It was established after the 1972 Shimla Agreement following the 1971 war.
9Which countries share India's northeast border?
💡 Explanation
India's northeast border is shared with China (Arunachal Pradesh, Sikkim), Bhutan, and Nepal. These three Himalayan countries form the northeastern boundary of India.
10India's eastern borders are shared with:
💡 Explanation
India's eastern borders are shared with Myanmar (in the far east) and Bangladesh. Myanmar borders the Seven Sister States while Bangladesh nearly encircles India's northeastern region.
🏔️ Section 2 — Peaks, Rivers & Geography
11What is the highest peak in India?
💡 Explanation
Kanchenjunga (28,169 ft / 8,586 m) is the highest peak in India, located on the border of Sikkim and Nepal. Mount Everest, though in the Himalayas, lies in Nepal, not India.
12The height of Kanchenjunga is:
💡 Explanation
Kanchenjunga stands at 28,169 ft (8,586 m) — the highest peak in India and the third highest in the world. Its name means "Five Treasures of Snow" in Tibetan, referring to its five peaks.
13Mount Everest is the highest peak on:
💡 Explanation
Mount Everest (29,032 ft / 8,849 m) is the highest peak on Earth — not just in Asia or Nepal. It is located in the Himalayan range on the Nepal-Tibet border. It is NOT in India.
14The Thar Desert is also known as:
💡 Explanation
The Thar Desert is officially called the Great Indian Desert. Located mainly in Rajasthan, it extends into parts of Pakistan. It is the world's 17th largest desert and the most densely populated desert.
15The Rann of Kutch is known for its:
💡 Explanation
The Rann of Kutch in Gujarat is famous for its unique salt marshes and wildlife. It is the world's largest salt desert and is home to the Indian wild ass. It becomes a seasonal wetland during monsoons.
16Which rivers flow through the Gangetic plains?
💡 Explanation
The Ganges and Brahmaputra are the two major rivers that flow through the Gangetic plains. The Ganges flows from west to east while Brahmaputra flows from east, both eventually meeting in Bangladesh.
17The Deccan Plateau is rich in which minerals?
💡 Explanation
The Deccan Plateau is rich in iron ore, coal, and bauxite. These minerals have made states like Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh major industrial centres in India.
18Which sea lies to the west of India?
💡 Explanation
The Arabian Sea lies to the west of India. The western coastal states of Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, and Kerala border the Arabian Sea, which connects India to the Middle East and Africa.
19Which water body lies to the east of India?
💡 Explanation
The Bay of Bengal lies to the east of India. Eastern coastal states like Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu border the Bay of Bengal, which is the world's largest bay.
20The Barak river flows in which region of India?
💡 Explanation
The Barak river flows in Northeast India, primarily through Assam and Manipur. Along with the Brahmaputra, it is one of the two key rivers of the Northeast region mentioned in the notes.
🏛️ Section 3 — Ancient Subcontinent
21The Indian subcontinent in ancient times included which countries?
💡 Explanation
The ancient Indian subcontinent comprised 8 countries: India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, Afghanistan, Myanmar, and Sri Lanka. These regions were culturally and geographically interconnected as one landmass.
22The rise of the Indus Valley Civilization, Maurya Empire, and Gupta Empire is associated with:
💡 Explanation
The Indo-Gangetic Plains — the heartland of ancient India — witnessed the rise of the Indus Valley Civilization, Maurya Empire, and Gupta Empire. Their fertile soil and major rivers supported dense populations and powerful kingdoms.
23Which passes were crucial trade routes in northwest India (now Pakistan)?
💡 Explanation
The Khyber and Bolan passes in the northwest (now Pakistan) were crucial trade and cultural exchange routes connecting the Indian subcontinent with Mesopotamia and Central Asia. Most ancient invaders and traders used these passes.
24The Greek chronicler Megasthenes wrote a work called:
💡 Explanation
Megasthenes, a Greek ambassador to the court of Chandragupta Maurya in the 4th century BCE, wrote Indica — one of the most valuable ancient accounts of India's geography, society, and political system.
25According to Megasthenes, which region was the only state dominated by females?
💡 Explanation
Megasthenes stated in Indica that Pandia — the ancient kingdom corresponding to modern Tamil Nadu — was the only state dominated by females. This is one of the earliest recorded references to matriarchal governance in India.
26Megasthenes belonged to which century BCE?
💡 Explanation
Megasthenes was a 4th century BCE Greek historian and diplomat. He served as the Seleucid ambassador at the court of Chandragupta Maurya (c. 302–298 BCE) and documented his observations in Indica.
27Ancient names of eastern region (now Bangladesh) include:
💡 Explanation
The ancient eastern region (now Bangladesh) was known by names like Vanga, Paundra, Radh, Gauda, and Samatata. These were distinct territories within the Bengal region, each with their own cultural and political identity.
28"New Found Land" or Nobodvip refers to a section of soil about 120 km north of:
💡 Explanation
"New Found Land" or Nobodvip refers to a section of soil approximately 120 km north of Kolkata. This fertile alluvial land was formed by the silt deposits of the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna river system.
29Which dynasties were prominent in the southern subcontinent?
💡 Explanation
The Cholas, Pallavas, and Chalukyas were the major dynasties of the southern subcontinent. They dominated the Deccan and South India, building powerful naval empires and influencing Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia.
30The fertile delta of the eastern region was formed by which rivers?
💡 Explanation
The fertile delta of eastern India (now Bangladesh) was formed by the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna river system — one of the world's largest river deltas. This makes the region extremely fertile but also flood-prone.
🌤️ Section 4 — Physical Environment & Seasons
31How many primary seasons are observed in north and central India?
💡 Explanation
North and central India observe four primary seasons: Winter (December–February), Spring (March), Summer (April–June), and Monsoon (June–September). This four-season pattern is distinct from coastal regions which have more moderate climates.
32The winter season in north and central India is:
💡 Explanation
Winter in north and central India runs from December to February. During this period, temperatures can drop significantly — especially in states like Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar where cold waves are common.
33Summer temperatures in plains can soar above:
💡 Explanation
Summer temperatures in Indian plains soar above 40°C. States like Rajasthan (Barmer, Jaisalmer), Delhi, and parts of UP regularly record temperatures of 45–48°C during peak summer months of May–June.
34The monsoon season in India is:
💡 Explanation
The monsoon season in India runs from June to September. The Southwest Monsoon enters India through Kerala in early June and covers the entire country by mid-July, bringing over 75% of India's annual rainfall.
35Which of the following is NOT one of the Seven Sister States?
💡 Explanation
Jharkhand is NOT one of the Seven Sister States. The Seven Sisters are: Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, and Tripura — all located in Northeast India. Jharkhand is in eastern India.
36The seven sister states are located in which region?
💡 Explanation
The Seven Sister States are located in Northeast India. They are called "sisters" due to their interdependence and similar socio-economic and cultural characteristics, and share borders with Bhutan, China, Myanmar, and Bangladesh.
🌿 Section 5 — Natural Environment & Biodiversity
37The Western Ghats are a:
💡 Explanation
The Western Ghats are designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site (since 2012). They run parallel to the western coast of India for about 1,600 km and are one of the world's eight "hottest hotspots" of biological diversity.
38The Western Ghats run parallel to which coast?
💡 Explanation
The Western Ghats run parallel to the western coast of India — from Gujarat in the north to Kerala in the south. They act as a barrier to the Southwest Monsoon, causing heavy rainfall on the western slopes.
39Which of the following is an endemic species found ONLY in Western Ghats?
💡 Explanation
The lion-tailed macaque is an endemic species found only in the Western Ghats. It is a critically endangered primate with a silvery-white mane. Bengal tiger and Asiatic water buffalo are found in other regions too.
40Which is also an endemic species of the Western Ghats?
💡 Explanation
The Nilgiri tahr is an endemic species of the Western Ghats. It is a wild mountain goat found only in the Nilgiri Hills and the higher parts of the Western Ghats. The Eravikulam National Park in Kerala is its stronghold.
41Kaziranga National Park is located in:
💡 Explanation
Kaziranga National Park is located in Assam, in the floodplains of the Brahmaputra River. It was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985 and is home to the world's largest population of one-horned rhinoceros.
42Kaziranga National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site famous for:
💡 Explanation
Kaziranga is famous for housing the world's largest population of one-horned rhinoceros (Indian rhinoceros). It also has the highest density of Bengal tigers among protected areas and hosts large numbers of Asian elephants.
43The Sundarbans is located in:
💡 Explanation
The Sundarbans is located in the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta — spanning India (West Bengal) and Bangladesh. The name "Sundarbans" comes from the Sundari trees (Heritiera fomes) that dominate this mangrove ecosystem.
44The Sundarbans is the world's:
💡 Explanation
The Sundarbans is the world's largest mangrove forest, covering about 10,000 sq km. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a critical habitat for the Royal Bengal tiger. The complex tidal waterways make it almost impenetrable.
45Which endangered species is found in the Sundarbans?
💡 Explanation
The endangered Gangetic dolphin is found in the Sundarbans. Also known as the South Asian River Dolphin, it is India's national aquatic animal. The Sundarbans also hosts estuarine crocodiles and Royal Bengal tigers.
46Chilika Lake is located in:
💡 Explanation
Chilika Lake is located in Odisha — it is Asia's largest brackish water lagoon and an important wetland for migratory birds. Millions of birds from Central Asia, Siberia, and other regions visit during winter.
47Keoladeo National Park (important for migratory birds) is located in:
💡 Explanation
Keoladeo National Park (also called Bharatpur Bird Sanctuary) is located in Rajasthan. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a critical wintering area for migratory birds including the endangered Siberian crane.
48Which island group has pristine coral reefs in the Arabian Sea?
💡 Explanation
The Lakshadweep Islands in the Arabian Sea are known for their pristine coral reefs. These 36 coral atolls host rich marine biodiversity. Andaman and Nicobar are in the Bay of Bengal, not the Arabian Sea.
49The Gulf of Mannar lies between:
💡 Explanation
The Gulf of Mannar lies between India and Sri Lanka. It is one of the world's richest marine biodiversity regions, known for coral reefs, seagrass meadows, and mangroves. It was declared one of India's first Marine Biosphere Reserves.
50Project Tiger was launched in:
💡 Explanation
Project Tiger was launched in 1973 by Prime Minister Indira Gandhi. It was India's first wildlife conservation initiative aimed specifically at protecting the Bengal tiger and its habitat. Tiger numbers have significantly recovered since then.
51The Namami Gange programme aims to:
💡 Explanation
The Namami Gange programme aims to restore and rejuvenate the River Ganges. It is a flagship programme of the Government of India that addresses pollution, restoration of the river's natural flow, and conservation of its biodiversity.
52The Northeast biodiversity hotspot is also called:
💡 Explanation
The Northeast biodiversity hotspot is called the Indo-Burma Biodiversity Hotspot. It covers states like Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, and Nagaland and is one of the world's 36 biodiversity hotspots with extraordinary species diversity.
⛏️ Section 6 — Jharkhand
53Jharkhand is located in:
💡 Explanation
Jharkhand is located in eastern India. It was carved out of Bihar on 15 November 2000 and is bordered by Bihar, West Bengal, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and Uttar Pradesh. Ranchi is its capital.
54Which plateau dominates Jharkhand?
💡 Explanation
The Chota Nagpur Plateau dominates Jharkhand, covering most of the state. It is part of the Peninsular Plateau of India and is one of the most mineral-rich regions in Asia — often called the "Ruhr of India."
55Which mountain range is in the North of Jharkhand?
💡 Explanation
Parasnath Hills are located in the north of Jharkhand. At 1,365 m, Parasnath is the highest point in Jharkhand and is considered the most sacred pilgrimage site for Jains, as 20 of the 24 Jain tirthankaras attained nirvana here.
56Which mountain range is in the West of Jharkhand?
💡 Explanation
Netarhat Hills are located in the west of Jharkhand. Known as the "Queen of Chotanagpur," Netarhat is a popular hill station at about 1,100 m altitude. It is famous for its sunrise and sunset views and dense forests.
57Jharkhand is especially known for which type of forest?
💡 Explanation
Jharkhand is especially known for its Sal (Shorea robusta) forests. Sal is one of the most important timber trees in India and is sacred to the tribal communities of Jharkhand. The forests also contain bamboo groves and medicinal plants.
58Which of the following rivers is NOT in Jharkhand?
💡 Explanation
The Barak river is NOT in Jharkhand — it flows in Northeast India, mainly through Assam and Manipur. The rivers of Jharkhand include Subarnarekha, Damodar (known as "Sorrow of Bengal"), and Koel.
59Jharkhand's mineral wealth includes all EXCEPT:
💡 Explanation
Petroleum is NOT found in Jharkhand. The state is rich in coal, iron ore, copper, mica, and bauxite — earning it the title of India's mineral heartland. Petroleum is found in states like Gujarat, Assam, and offshore in Maharashtra.
60The crops grown in Jharkhand include:
💡 Explanation
Jharkhand grows paddy, wheat, maize, pulses, and oilseeds as its main crops. The state has a predominantly agrarian rural economy alongside its mining sector. Paddy is the most important kharif crop of the region.
